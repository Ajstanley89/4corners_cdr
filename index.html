Project Information

The Four Corners Carbon Solutions (FCCS) facility is a regional Direct Air Capture and Storage (DACS) hub designed to remove CO‚ÇÇ from the atmosphere and permanently store it underground. We operate with transparency, sustainability, and public engagement at the core of our mission.

Project Timeline
  • 2023 ‚Äì Feasibility Study: Site evaluation, tribal consultation, and geological analysis completed.
  • 2024 ‚Äì Permitting & Design: Environmental impact assessment submitted; engineering and permitting finalized.
  • 2025 ‚Äì Construction: Facility and pipeline infrastructure under construction.
  • 2026 ‚Äì Operations Begin: CO‚ÇÇ capture units go live with injection testing underway.
  • 2027 and Beyond: Full-scale operations, ongoing monitoring, and public reporting.
Permitting & Regulatory Overview
  • Class VI UIC Permit: Issued by the EPA for geologic CO‚ÇÇ storage; ensures long-term containment and public safety.
  • NEPA Compliance: Environmental assessment and tribal consultations completed under federal guidelines.
  • Tribal Coordination: Ongoing engagement with Navajo Nation and Ute Mountain Ute Tribe on land, water, and cultural impacts.
  • Monitoring, Reporting, Verification (MRV): Regular data reporting to meet DOE and EPA requirements.

Frequently Asked Questions

Below are answers to common questions we hear from residents, partners, and stakeholders in the Four Corners region. If you don‚Äôt see your question listed, reach out to us ‚Äî we‚Äôd love to hear from you.

üß≠ Why should I care about carbon capture?

Carbon dioxide (CO‚ÇÇ) is a major contributor to climate change, and carbon capture directly removes it from the atmosphere. This helps reduce wildfires, water shortages, and extreme weather while creating local jobs and driving new investment in clean energy infrastructure.

üåç Why should we do this here?

The Four Corners region is ideal for carbon removal because of its geology, solar energy resources, and existing workforce. It allows us to create climate solutions rooted in community benefit and environmental integrity.

üåé Will this cause earthquakes?

No. Our CO‚ÇÇ storage is done in deep saline formations far below drinking water aquifers, and we follow strict EPA Class VI permitting rules. The volumes and pressures are carefully managed to avoid induced seismicity, and the site is continuously monitored.

üìê How much land is needed for this project?

Stage 1 of the project uses approximately 35‚Äì50 acres for the capture facility, pipelines, and monitoring stations. The underground storage formation spans a much larger area, but all activity on the surface is limited and controlled.

üíß How much water is needed?

For this phase of the project, water use is approximately 1,000‚Äì1,500 gallons per day ‚Äî comparable to a small commercial operation. We use dry cooling and are actively exploring reclaimed water options to minimize freshwater use.

üèòÔ∏è Does this benefit the local community? How?

Yes. The project creates skilled jobs, supports tribal and educational partnerships, contributes to regional climate resilience, and prioritizes local sourcing. We also provide workforce training and STEM outreach in schools.

üîß What trades are being hired?

We're hiring electricians, welders, equipment operators, technicians, environmental monitors, and community engagement staff. See our Jobs section for openings and training pathways.

üìÖ When is the next community engagement event?

We‚Äôre hosting a regional update in Farmington, NM in Fall 2025, with additional events by request. Stay informed by joining our mailing list or visiting the Community Involvement section.

üõ†Ô∏è What does decommissioning look like?

At the end of its operational life, the site will be safely decommissioned. All infrastructure is removed or made inert, wells are sealed, and monitoring continues for 50+ years to ensure environmental safety. The land can return to other uses or open space.
